# Regularity versus Naturalness: The Wahl–Jespersen Discussion on an International Language (1935)

## Summary

The discussion is organized into five sections, followed by a joint conclusion.

**I. Introduction.** The two authors first stress their common ground: both belong to the naturalistic school and reject purely a priori projects (Ro), the distortions of Volapük, and Esperanto's artificial features (its pronominal table, invented words like *edzo*, the rigid system of endings, and the circumflexed letters) — though both praise Zamenhof's invented preposition *ye*. They then state their guiding principles: de Wahl demands that an auxiliary language be an organic, autonomous whole based on the international forms common to European languages, comprehensible almost without prior instruction; Jespersen places ease of learning highest, demanding maximal regularity and simplicity while acknowledging internationality and expressiveness.

**II. Alphabet.** De Wahl criticizes Novial's earlier replacement of *c* and *z* with *k* and *s* as destroying etymological coherence. Jespersen, a lifelong phonetician, defends the phonetic rationale but concedes that the public was shocked by it and has now reintroduced *c* and *z*, while keeping *k* in words like *ke* and *kelki*. They differ on *qu*, the guttural *ch*, and the double pronunciation of *g*, which Occidental accepts (as in French) and Novial rejects; de Wahl regards much of this as a matter of aesthetics — the public is won "more by the eye than by the ear."

**III. Derivation.** De Wahl argues that Esperanto and Ido cannot regularly derive the international vocabulary (*-ion, -iv, -ura, -ntie*, etc.), hiding thousands of exceptions in the lexicon, and criticizes Novial's tendency to force verbs into the *a*-class against their natural derivatives. Jespersen admits the point and promises to review his lexicon, but counters that Occidental offers too many competing suffixes for verbal nouns (he lists ten) and seven methods of forming verbs from adjectives, which must confuse learners; he defends Novial's regular suffixes *-o* and *-eso*. De Wahl replies that this diversity mirrors natural languages and that analogy, not rigid logic, is how the public actually learns.

**IV. Flexion.** Both languages share plural *-s*, comparison with *plu/minu*, the optional adjective ending *-i*, and auxiliary-based conjugation. De Wahl criticizes Novial's *ha ama* (auxiliary plus bare stem) and the overloaded verbal theme; Jespersen announces he had already decided to abandon that system in favor of forms like *ha convictet*, and accepts an optional infinitive in *-r*.

**V. Pronouns, adverbs, negation.** De Wahl defends distinct case forms (*tu/te*) and his possessives *nor, vor* as anticipated natural evolution, while rejecting Novial's *nusen, vusen* and the *-n* genitive as artificial. Jespersen notes his optional accusative *-m* proved unnecessary in practice, defends his regular three-gender system (*-e, -o, -a*, plus neuter *-um*) against Occidental's single *su*, and argues for *non* over *ne* as the negative, citing his own research on negation. De Wahl answers that the most frequent words are irregular but fluent in all languages, and that international words in *ín-* cannot simply be abolished.

**Conclusion.** Both authors agree that Novial emphasizes systematic regularity and Occidental elastic convenience, and that the future international language will be a practical synthesis of both principles — "not very far from Novial and Occidental."

## The Two Positions

**Otto Jespersen (Novial)** puts *ease of learning* above all else. Since national languages are hard precisely because of their irregularities and idioms, an international language must offer the highest possible degree of regularity and simplicity: one all-purpose noun suffix (*-o*), a regular abstract suffix (*-eso*), possessives formed mechanically with *-n*, a systematic three-gender scheme (*-e, -o, -a*, neuter *-um*), and as few derivational devices as possible — "every superfluity is bad." As a phonetician he initially favored strictly phonetic spelling, and his test is always the learner who knows no French, English, or Spanish and therefore cannot fall back on familiarity. He treats his language as revisable: during the discussion he reports having already given up phonetic *k/s* spelling and the *ha ama* construction when experience showed them to be obstacles.

**Edgar de Wahl (Occidental)** puts *naturalness* above all else. An auxiliary language must behave like a natural language — an organic, autonomous whole obeying the common laws of European languages — so that it is readable almost without instruction and does not shock the public with invented forms. Where Jespersen prizes one regular rule, de Wahl prizes faithful reproduction of the international word stock: the many coexisting suffixes (*-ion, -ntie, -ura, -ment*, ...) are not a defect but a facilitation, because speakers choose by analogy with their own language, and "the psychological law demands regularity only in unknown things" — the most frequent words in all languages are irregular but fluent. He consistently rejects forms found in no natural language (*nusen*, *lon/lan*, *-eso*, *ha* + bare stem), even when they are perfectly regular.

In short, both accept regularity *and* naturalness as requirements; they differ on which one yields when the two conflict. Jespersen resolves conflicts in favor of the learner who must actively master the system; de Wahl resolves them in favor of the reader who must understand it at first sight.
